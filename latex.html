Converting Systems of Equations to Matrices

[t][.5in][t]2.4in Consider the following system of two simultaneous linear equations. How might you convert these equations into matrix form? \[\systeme{ -8x-3y=-19, 16x-8y=24 }\]

[t][.5in][t]2.4in In order to convert those equations into a matrix we must strip away the letters and use a matrix form called an augmented matrix:

\( \begin{bmatrix}[*1cr@{\quad}|@{\quad}>{\color{black}}r] -8 & -3 & -19 \\ 16 & -8 & 24 \end{bmatrix} \)

How to Solve Systems Using Gaussian Elimination

Focus on the left side of that augmented matrix for a minute.

[t][.5in][t]2.4in What you want to do is play around with the rows of that augmented matrix until the four numbers on the left side look like this:

[t][.5in][t]1in

\( \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \)

[t][.5in][t]2.4in This is called an identity matrix. If you can make the left side of your matrix look like this, you will have solved it for both variables.

How do you do this? You manipulate the entire rows using what are called elementary row operations. This is much easier than it looks. Watch this:

[t][.5in][t]2.5in Here is our augmented matrix again:

\( \begin{bmatrix}[*1cr@{\quad}|@{\quad}>{\color{black}}r] -8 & -3 & -19 \\ 16 & -8 & 24 \end{bmatrix} \)

[t][.5in][t]2.4in What if we divided the entire bottom row by \(2\) then added it to the top row, replacing the bottom row with the resulting sum?

[t][.5in][t]2.4in Step 1: Divide the whole bottom row by \(2\):

\( \begin{bmatrix}[*1cr@{\quad}|@{\quad}>{\color{black}}r] -8 & -3 & -19 \\ \frac{16}{2} & \frac{-8}{2} & \frac{24}{2} \end{bmatrix} \)

[t][.7in][t]3in

Step 2: (intermediate step): Simplify.

\( \begin{bmatrix}[*1cr@{\quad}|@{\quad}>{\color{black}}r] -8 & -3 & -19 \\ 8 & -4 & 12 \end{bmatrix} \)

[t][.7in][t]3in

Step 3: Add new bottom row to top row: \( \begin{bmatrix}[*1cr@{\quad}|@{\quad}>{\color{black}}r] -8 & -3 & -19 \\ ({8+{-8}}) & ({-4+{-3}}) & ({12+{-19}}) \end{bmatrix} \)

[t][.7in][t]3in

Step 4: (intermediate step): Simplify. \( \begin{bmatrix}[*1cr@{\quad}|@{\quad}>{\color{black}}r] -8 & -3 & -19 \\ 0 & -7 & 7 \end{bmatrix} \)

[t][.7in][t]3in

Step 5: Divide entire bottom row by \(-7\). \( \begin{bmatrix}[*1cr@{\quad}|@{\quad}>{\color{black}}r] -8 & -3 & -19 \\ \frac{0}{-7} & \frac{-7}{-7} & \frac{7}{-7} \end{bmatrix} \)

[t][.7in][t]3in

Step 6: (intermediate step): Simplify. \( \begin{bmatrix}[*1cr@{\quad}|@{\quad}>{\color{black}}r] -8 & -3 & -19 \\ 0 & 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \)

[t][.7in][t]3in

Step 7: Add the bottom row to the top. \( \begin{bmatrix}[*1cr@{\quad}|@{\quad}>{\color{black}}r] ({-8+0}) & ({-3+1}) & ({-19+1}) \\ 0 & 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \)

[t][.7in][t]3in

Step 8: (intermediate step): Simplify. \( \begin{bmatrix}[*1cr@{\quad}|@{\quad}>{\color{black}}r] -8 & -2 & -18 \\ 0 & 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \)

[t][.7in][t]3in

Step 9: Divide top row by \(-2\). \( \begin{bmatrix}[*1cr@{\quad}|@{\quad}>{\color{black}}r] \frac{-8}{-2} & \frac{-2}{-2} & \frac{-18}{-2} \\ 0 & 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \)

[t][.7in][t]3in

Step 10: (intermediate step): Simplify. \( \begin{bmatrix}[*1cr@{\quad}|@{\quad}>{\color{black}}r] 4 & 1 & 9 \\ 0 & 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \)

[t][.7in][t]3in

Step 11: Subtract the bottom row from the top. \( \begin{bmatrix}[*1cr@{\quad}|@{\quad}>{\color{black}}r] ({4-0}) & ({1-1}) & ({9-1}) \\ 0 & 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \)

[t][.7in][t]3in

Step 12: (intermediate step): Simplify. \( \begin{bmatrix}[*1cr@{\quad}|@{\quad}>{\color{black}}r] 4 & 0 & 8 \\ 0 & 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \)

[t][.7in][t]3in

Step 13: Divide the top row by \(4\). \( \begin{bmatrix}[*1cr@{\quad}|@{\quad}>{\color{black}}r] \frac{4}{4} & \frac{0}{4} & \frac{8}{4} \\ 0 & 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \)

[t][.7in][t]3in

FINAL STEP: Simplify. You’re done! \( \begin{bmatrix}[*1cr@{\quad}|@{\quad}>{\color{black}}r] 1 & 0 & 2 \\ 0 & 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \)

Since the left side is the identity matrix \({I}={ \left(\begin{smallmatrix}1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1\end{smallmatrix}\right)}\) then \(x=2\) and \(y=1\). Thus, the solution to the system of equations is \((\coord{2,1})\).

You just performed something amazing called Gaussian elimination in order to reduce the original augmented matrix to a new form called reduced row echelon form. This technique was invented by Carl Friedrich Gauss (1777–1855). It’s extraordinarily powerful in what it can do with really simple arithmetic.

You can do the same operations on multiple additional equations, including two, three, or more multiple variable equations too!

Here are the three rules of Gaussian elimination:

  1. You can multiply a row by a non-zero number (negative numbers and fractions are allowed).

  2. You can add one row to another row or to a multiple of another row.

  3. You can swap the positions of any two rows.
